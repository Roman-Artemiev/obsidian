# Hooks

Hooks on LinkedIn should create clarity plus tension, not mystery for its own sake.

## What A Good Hook Does

- Signals relevance fast
- Creates a reason to continue
- Implies a payoff
- Stays believable

## Strong Hook Types

### 1. Contrarian Truth

Use when challenging a common professional assumption.

Examples:

- Most LinkedIn posts fail before the second paragraph.
- More reach is not the same thing as more demand.
- The problem is not that your audience is too small. It is that your point is too vague.

### 2. Specific Observation

Use when you noticed a repeatable pattern.

Examples:

- The best event marketers I know do one thing before they publish any launch post.
- After rewriting dozens of founder posts, the same structural mistake keeps showing up.
- Most “personal brand” posts collapse because the lesson appears too late.

### 3. Outcome With Tension

Use when there is a credible result and a reason it is surprising.

Examples:

- We changed one section of the post template and replies doubled.
- A shorter post outperformed the detailed version, but not for the reason we expected.
- One document post generated more qualified conversations than a month of generic updates.

### 4. Mistake Or Friction

Use when the reader is likely making the same mistake.

Examples:

- If your LinkedIn post needs six lines to say what it is about, the draft is not ready.
- The fastest way to lose attention is to hide the point behind a long setup.
- Many good ideas die on LinkedIn because they were written like essays instead of posts.

### 5. Sharp Lesson

Use when you can compress the insight into one line.

Examples:

- People do not engage with effort. They engage with clarity.
- Credibility on LinkedIn comes from specificity, not polish.
- A good post does not sound smart first. It becomes useful first.

## Hook Rules

- Do not oversell the payoff.
- Avoid fake suspense like “I learned something shocking today.”
- Avoid broad empty claims like “content is changing.”
- Avoid opening with biography unless the post truly depends on it.
- If the hook can fit any platform, it is probably too generic for LinkedIn.

## Quick Hook Test

Ask:

- Would the right reader know this is for them?
- Does the body pay off the promise quickly?
- Is the claim concrete enough to trust?
- Is the tension real, not manufactured?
