# Platform

This file captures the platform-specific constraints and behavior that matter when writing for LinkedIn.

## Current Working Constraints

- Standard LinkedIn posts support up to `3,000` characters according to LinkedIn Help.
- LinkedIn supports several native formats from the post composer:
  `post`, `video`, `photo`, `article`, `poll`, and `document`.
- LinkedIn newsletters exist for members and Pages and are best treated as recurring long-form publishing, not as ordinary posts.

## What The Feed Rewards

Based on LinkedIn engineering posts from `May 12, 2020`, `October 1, 2024`, and `March 12, 2026`:

- Feed ranking is heavily relevance-driven.
- LinkedIn uses dwell time and skip behavior as signals, not just overt engagement.
- The system increasingly tries to understand what a post is actually about and whether it matches a member’s professional interests and goals.

Practical implication:

- The opening must earn attention honestly.
- The body must sustain it.
- Empty hooks, bait, and vague “inspirational” text are risky because they create skips.

## Writing Implications

- Make the topic obvious early.
- Make the post feel professionally useful, timely, or insightful.
- Use formatting that helps reading rather than gaming the feed.
- Deliver on the opening fast.
- Match the topic to the right audience instead of writing to everyone.

## LinkedIn-Native Behavior

- Posts compete in a professional context, so relevance usually beats pure entertainment.
- Comments matter because LinkedIn is conversation-heavy, but comments alone should not be the design goal.
- Native formats usually deserve preference over sending people away too early.
- Newsletters are useful when the promise is recurring and topic-led.
- Document posts are useful when the content is step-based, slide-based, or framework-heavy.

## Practical Guardrails

- If the draft exceeds `3,000` characters, convert it to an article or compress it.
- If the idea depends on diagrams, steps, or swipeable frames, consider a document post.
- If the goal is repeated education on a stable topic, consider a newsletter.
- If the first two lines are generic, rewrite before touching the rest of the draft.
- If the value only appears at the end, restructure the piece.
