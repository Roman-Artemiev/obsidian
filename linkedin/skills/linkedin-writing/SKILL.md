# LinkedIn Writing

## Overview

Use this skill to turn a rough idea into a strong LinkedIn draft.

Optimize for four things:

1. Professional relevance
2. Fast comprehension
3. Credible specificity
4. Readability that sustains attention

## Workflow

1. Identify the goal:
   lead generation, authority, hiring, event promotion, learning, opinion, story, or conversation.
2. Identify the best format before writing:
   post, article, newsletter, document, poll, or mixed post + asset.
   Read [references/formats.md](references/formats.md) when format choice is not obvious.
3. Define the audience:
   role, seniority, problem, and what they should think, feel, or do after reading.
4. Build the core message:
   one idea, one tension, one takeaway.
5. Draft the opening:
   read [references/hooks.md](references/hooks.md) when the first lines are weak or generic.
6. Shape the body for scanability:
   read [references/structure.md](references/structure.md).
7. Tune the voice:
   read [references/style.md](references/style.md) to keep the draft modern, useful, and human.
8. Check platform fit:
   confirm the draft fits LinkedIn mechanics in [references/platform.md](references/platform.md).
9. End with one clear action:
   comment, follow, connect, click, subscribe, register, or reflect.

## Core Rules

- Start with substance, not throat-clearing.
- Do not stack multiple ideas in one post unless the format explicitly supports it.
- Prefer short paragraphs, but do not force every sentence onto its own line.
- Replace abstractions with observations, examples, numbers, contrasts, or consequences.
- Avoid engagement bait, vague inspiration, and recycled “thought leadership” phrasing.
- Write for professional usefulness, not generic virality.
- If the post teaches, make the takeaway easy to extract.
- If the post sells, lead with relevance and insight before the ask.

## Drafting Defaults

- Default to `hook -> context -> insight -> implication -> CTA`.
- Keep paragraphs compact enough to scan on mobile.
- Use lists only when the content is genuinely list-shaped.
- Prefer one CTA, not several competing asks.
- If a claim sounds strong, support it with an example, mechanism, or data point.

## Output Modes

- **Write from scratch:** produce 2-3 hook options, then one full draft.
- **Rewrite:** preserve the idea, improve hook, structure, and clarity.
- **Critique:** evaluate hook, structure, credibility, relevance, and CTA.
- **Repurpose:** convert between post, article, newsletter intro, event promo, or document post.

## Common Mistakes

- Opening with “I’m excited to share...”
- Writing a hook that promises more tension than the body delivers
- Using line breaks as a substitute for structure
- Sounding profound instead of being clear
- Teaching without giving a concrete takeaway
- Telling a story without stating why it matters to the reader
- Asking for comments when there is nothing meaningful to comment on
