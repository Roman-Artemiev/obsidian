
## Phase 0 — Define the hypothesis (1–2 hours)

Before talking to anyone, write this clearly:

> “I believe [specific people] have a problem with [specific situation], and it causes [pain], because [current solution is insufficient].”

I believe people who organize events struggle to share photos on time and it cause a lack of marketing and unsolved potential because there is not platform for it

### Output:
- Target user (very specific)
- Problem
- Why it matters

Example:

> “Freelance designers struggle to manage client feedback because it’s scattered across emails, Figma, and messages, causing missed changes and wasted time.”

---

## Phase 1 — Market scan (1–2 hours)

Goal: confirm the problem already exists in some form.

Do:
- Search Reddit, forums, Discords
- Look for complaints (“how do I…”, “is there tool for…”, “pain with…”)
- Check existing tools

### You’re looking for:
- repeated frustration
- existing workarounds
- people hacking solutions together

### Output:
- 5–10 real quotes or complaints

If nothing exists → risk is higher (not always bad, but important signal)

---

## Phase 2 — Recruit 5–15 people (1–3 days)
Find people who:
- actually experience the problem (not random users)
- are not your friends if possible

Where to find them:
- Reddit communities
- LinkedIn
- Discord groups
- Twitter/X
- niche Slack groups

Message template:
> “Hey, I’m researching how people handle [problem]. Could I ask you 10–15 min questions? I’m not selling anything.”

---

## Phase 3 — Problem interviews (core step)

Goal: understand reality, not opinions.

### Rules:
❌ don’t pitch your idea  
❌ don’t ask “would you use this?”  
✔ focus on past behavior

### Questions:
- “Walk me through how you currently do X”
- “When was the last time this happened?”
- “What was frustrating about it?”
- “What tools do you use?”
- “What did you try before that didn’t work?”
- “What happens if you don’t solve it?”

### Output:
For each person:
- pain level (1–10)
- frequency
- current workaround

---

## Phase 4 — Pattern detection (1–2 hours)

Now you analyze:

### Strong signal looks like:

- same problem mentioned by 6+ people
- emotional frustration (“annoying”, “waste of time”, “hate it”)
- clear workaround exists but is inefficient

### Weak signal:

- everyone describes different problems
- low urgency
- “nice to have” only

---

## Phase 5 — Problem validation test (no product yet)

Now you test willingness to change.

Show:

- simple text description OR Figma mock OR landing page

Ask:

- “Would you use something like this next week?”
- “What would you replace to use this?”
- “Would you sign up for early access?”

### Strong signal:

- they want to try it immediately
- they suggest features themselves
- they ask when it’s ready

---

## Phase 6 — Commitment test (very important)

Don’t rely on words. Get action.

Pick one:

- waitlist signup
- email collection
- “I’ll let you try beta”
- pre-order (if relevant)

### Rule:

👉 If nobody commits, demand is weak

---

## Phase 7 — MVP build (only after validation)

Now build smallest possible version:

### Checklist:

- solves ONE core problem
- usable in <1 minute
- no extra features

Avoid:

- dashboards
- settings
- complexity

Think: “single-use tool”

---

## Phase 8 — Real usage test (truth phase)

Put it in hands of users:

Track:

- do they return?
- do they use it more than once?
- do they rely on it without reminders?

### Strong signal:

- organic repeat usage
- users complain when it breaks
- users recommend it

---

# 🧠 Decision rule (simple)

After Phase 5–8:

### Continue if:

- repeated pain (5+ users)
- clear urgency
- people commit actions
- early usage sticks

### Stop if:

- interest but no action
- vague problem
- no repetition across users

---

# ⚡ Simple version (TL;DR flow)

1. Define hypothesis
2. Check online signals
3. Interview 5–15 users
4. Find repeating pain
5. Test willingness (mock/landing)
6. Get commitment (waitlist / signup)
7. Build tiny MVP
8. Measure real usage
